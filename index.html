Research Framework & Model

The PhD investigates the financial transmission chain from physical hazard to systemic bank risk:

Flooding, drought-induced foundation subsidence, soil degradation
→
02
Property Value Decline
Market repricing as climate risk becomes visible in transaction prices
→
03
LTV Ratio Breach
Mortgage balance exceeds collateral value — homeowner is underwater
→
04
Homeowner Distress
Negative equity, refinancing difficulty, forced sale or default
→
05
Portfolio Default Risk
Concentrated losses erode bank capital buffers — systemic exposure

LTV Stress Model

For each municipality, a climate discount is applied to current average property values: discount = αf·flood + αd·foundation + αdr·drought + αh·heat + αp·pluvial. The stressed LTV shifts as LTV* = LTV₀ / (1 − discount), capped at 60%. The national distribution is a weighted mixture of municipality Gaussians (σ = 18 pp). Flood risk: clamp(0.68 − elevNAP × 0.10, 0.05, 0.95) from AHN4. Heat risk proxied from urban density (UHI severity). Pluvial risk from impervious surface cover and terrain drainage capacity.

PhD Research Direction

This demo implements a static stress model. The PhD research will replace this with a dynamic Agent-Based Model (building on RHEA and Bank of England ABMs) incorporating behavioural responses from homeowners, banks, and investors — capturing feedback loops between price expectations, lending standards, and realised defaults over the mortgage term (2025–2055).

Current Model Limitations & Research Roadmap

This demo is an honest approximation. Every simplification below represents a concrete research task for the PhD.

⚠

No per-property mortgage data

The demo applies the national average LTV (72%) uniformly to every building. In reality, ~45% of Dutch residential buildings are social housing (woningcorporaties) or private rental — these have no individual mortgage exposure. Even among owner-occupied homes, ~32% are mortgage-free.

Current proxy Unit count from BAG — 1-unit buildings ≈ 68% mortgage probability; 50+ units ≈ 4% (woningcorporatie)
PhD data CBS microdata environment: address-level linked property × ownership × mortgage registry (standard Dutch university access)
⚠

Discount factors (α) are calibrated, not estimated

The αflood, αfoundation etc. multipliers are set from literature (Bernstein et al. 2019, DNB 2021) rather than estimated econometrically from Dutch transaction data. The true hedonic discount for Dutch flood risk is contested — some studies find 3–8%, others find no statistically significant effect due to flood-risk myopia.

PhD method Hedonic regression on Kadaster transaction data (2010–2024) with spatial RD around flood-zone boundaries to identify causal price impacts
⚠

Municipality-centroid elevation, not per-building

AHN4 elevation is queried at the municipality centroid — a single point representing ~340 km² in rural areas. Within Amsterdam alone, elevation ranges from −5 m (deep Watergraafsmeer polder) to +4 m (Amsteldijk). Two buildings 500 m apart can have radically different flood exposure.

Current state AHN4 real elevation for 339/345 municipalities — vastly better than province-level hand-coding
PhD data Per-building AHN4 query using BAG centroid coordinates; or join with Risicokaart flood-depth rasters (25 m resolution) directly
⚠

Static model — no feedback dynamics

The stress test shifts LTV in one step with no behavioural responses: no bank deleveraging, no panic selling, no insurance withdrawal, no expectation formation. In practice, once climate risk becomes visible in prices, a feedback loop between declining collateral, tighter lending standards, and further price falls can amplify losses non-linearly.

PhD model Agent-Based Model (RHEA-NL): heterogeneous homeowners, banks, and investors interact over 2025–2055; captures fire-sale externalities and endogenous credit tightening

Data sources unlocked at PhD stage

  • CBS microdata (Remote Access) — Address-level linked property value, ownership type, mortgage amount, household income. Standard access for TU Delft PhDs.
  • DNB AnaCredit — Loan-level mortgage data reported by Dutch banks under ECB regulation. Requires DNB research collaboration agreement.
  • Kadaster transaction data — All NL property sales 2000–present with full address, price, and buyer/seller. Available via CBS microdata linkage.
  • BRO geotechnical boreholes — Per-address soil profiles (peat depth, groundwater level) for precise foundation risk. Freely available via PDOK.
  • KNMI'23 scenarios at 1 km — High-resolution climate projections to 2100 under W, Wh, G, Gh scenarios. Published 2023, openly available.
  • NHG portfolio data — National Mortgage Guarantee risk classifications by postcode; available through NHG research partnership.
→

Known unknowns not yet modelled

  • Insurance withdrawal — Insurers are already non-renewing flood policies in parts of Zeeland and Groningen. Uninsurability accelerates price correction beyond the physical damage channel.
  • Renovation & adaptation — Flood-proofing, heat pump retrofits, and foundation reinforcement can reduce risk but require capital. Current model treats buildings as static.
  • Social housing stock — Woningcorporaties have €200B+ in bonds. Climate-related impairment of their collateral poses systemic risk via a different channel (not individual LTV but institutional solvency).
  • Spatial correlation — When flood risk materialises, many mortgages fail simultaneously (correlated defaults). A Gaussian mixture model understates tail dependence.
  • Risk myopia & disclosure lag — Dutch buyers currently do not receive mandatory flood-risk disclosure at point of sale (unlike the US NFIP). Pricing may correct abruptly once disclosure becomes mandatory.
